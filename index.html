Chapter One 4.5B Years

Born from catastrophe.

Four and a half billion years ago, a young Earth collided with a wandering protoplanet — a body the size of Mars — in a cataclysm that shook the solar system. Molten debris was flung into orbit.

Gravity pulled the shattered fragments together. Over thousands of years the rock cooled, solidified, and found its shape. Not from stillness — but from the most spectacular collision our solar system had ever seen.

Chapter Two World Mythology

She was given
a thousand names.

Ancient peoples looked up and saw a goddess. The Greeks called her Selene, riding her silver chariot across the night sky. The Romans named her Luna. The Chinese told of Chang'e, who drank an elixir of immortality and floated to the moon forever.

In Japan, Tsukuyomi was the moon god, banished to walk the night — which is why the sun and moon never share the same sky. Every culture, on every continent, looked up and saw something divine.

"The moon does not fight. It attacks no one. It keeps to its course, but by its very nature, it gently influences what is around it.

— Deng Ming-Dao

Chapter Three July 20, 1969

We went to see
the Moon and found
the Earth.

Two human beings pressed their boots into lunar regolith and left footprints that will remain for a million years. There is no wind up there to erase them. No rain, no erosion — only silence and the slow passage of time.

Looking up, I saw Earth — a small blue marble, impossibly fragile, hanging alone in an infinite black. The universe was vast and cold. And there was home.

In Full Glory

Every crater tells
a billion-year-old story.

The scars on her surface are the records of an ancient solar system in chaos. Each impact basin — Tycho, Copernicus, Mare Tranquillitatis — holds the memory of a collision from the deep past of time.

Epilogue

She
Remains.

Every night, without fail, she rises. She has watched empires crumble and forests grow from ash. She has illuminated every love letter ever written, every child born into darkness, every ship searching for home.

She will be there tonight.
Look up.
